Relative Clauses

A relative clause is a clause that describes a noun. In English, “who is talking” in “The man who talks is happy.” describes “the man”. In Latin, we use “qui” to usually start relative clauses, corresponding to “who/which/that.”

Returning to our previous example, “The man who talks is happy” in Latin is “vir qui inquit laeta est” (note that qui agrees with the masculine singular vir.) That being said, the relative pronoun must agree in both gender and number with its antecedent. However, the case and gender (and even number) of the relative pronoun is not always nominative in every situation, and could be used in times like “vir quem audio” (the man who I hear).

Indirect Statement

A direct statement would be a statement like “canem vidimus” (we saw the dog). If my friend (say that he’s a boy) says that we saw the dog, then we have an indirect statement.

An indirect statement is made from a main verb (saying, perceiving, or thinking) plus an accusative plus an infinitive. Thus, the translation of “He says that we saw the dog” becomes “dicit nos canem vidisse”, where “dicit” is the main verb, “nos” is the accusative, and “vidisse” is the infinitive (here, in the perfect tense.) The infinitive doesn’t change the main verb’s tense - only the action the infinitive says. (That is, changing “vidisse” to “videre” will not result in changing “says”, only “saw” to “see” again.)

The main verb is usually a verb of saying, thinking, or perceiving, like “puto” (I think), “cogito” (I think), “scio” (I know), “dico” (I say), “narro” (I tell), “video” (I see), and “sentio” (I feel).

And that’s it.
