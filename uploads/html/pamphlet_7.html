4. Ablative Place Where

  1. in urbe sunt
    “they are in the city”
  2. Denotes everything else not covered by the locative.

5. Ablative of Comparison

  1. melior fratre sum!
    “I am better than [my] brother”

6. Ablative of Means

  1. Another term for ablative of instrument

7. Ablative of Absolute

  1. hostibus victīs, discesserunt
    “with the enemies having been defeated, they left”
  2. Noun and participle in the ablative
  3. Denotes another clause in the sentence

8. Vocative of Direct Address

  1. amice, veni!
    “friend, come!”
  2. Used to address someone directly.

9. Accusative of Indirect Address

  1. O immortales deos…
    “O[h] immortal gods…”
  2. Used to address someone or something not with or near the speaker.

10. Dative of Possession

  1. mihi malum est
    “I have an apple”
    Literally “an apple is to me”
  2. sum and a dative used

11. Ablative of Object of the Preposition

  1. ex hortõ venis
    “You come from the garden.”
  2. Used where a preposition requires its object to be in the ablative case

More Functions

I covered the following functions last time: nominative of subject, genitive of possession, genitive of partitive, genitive of value, dative of indirect object, dative of reference, dative of impersonal construction, accusative of direct object, accusative of time, double accusative, ablative of instrument, and ablative of agent. This time, I’ll cover a few more.

  1. Ablative Place from Which
  1. ab Romā venimus
    “we come from Rome”
  2. Denotes an action away from something

2. Accusative Place to Which

  1. Galliam eunt
    “they go to Gaul”
  2. Denotes an action to something

3. Locative Place Where

  1. Romae estis
    “you (all) come in Rome”
  2. Denotes the name of a small city, island, or town, and other exceptions (domus, rus, humus, etc.)

12. Accusative of Object of the Preposition

  1. post mortem
    “after death”
  2. Used where a preposition requires its object to be in the ablative case.

Note: for both #11 and #12, refer to word list 3b in pamphlet 6 for some prepositions and their cases.

13. Ablative of Accompaniment

  1. cum puerïs
    “with the boys”
  2. Used to show accompaniment with someone else
