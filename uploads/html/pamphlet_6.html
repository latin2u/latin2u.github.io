[Present Active] Participles

We’ve covered nouns, adjectives, some verbs, adverbs, and some pronouns. We’ll move on to participles this time.

Before we talk about participles, we’ll introduce verbals, which are verbs that “dress up” as other parts of speech. They include gerunds, participles, and infinitives. You already know what infinitives are  - they are in the form of “to [verb].” Gerunds are verbs disguised as nouns, like “running”, “typing” (what I’m doing now), but here, they are still irrelevant. What matters are participles - verbs disguised as adjectives, as in “the running girl surpassed all the boys” and “the cheering crowd made him feel joyful.”

Latin also has participles, gerunds, and infinitives. We’ll talk about gerunds later in another issue, but we’ll start with participles. Since participles are derived from verbs, we’ll start with an easy one: voco, vocare, vocavi, vocatus meaning to call.

Take the second principal part and find the root, which is “voca”. Add “nt” to give “vocant”, which will be your new root. Then add modified i-stem third declension endings. The ablative could end in i/e, and the nominative will be the root, but ending in s instead of t.

The Future Tense

We’ve covered quite a bit with verbs, but we’re not in the future yet. This time, we’ll cover another important tense: the future tense!

As its name implies, this tense is used to mention the future, as in “she will do this” and “you shall not pass.” (I’ve also used “we’ll” and “we will” multiple times during this pamphlet.)
